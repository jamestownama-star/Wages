JAMESTOWN — OPEN GROUND — NIGHT (COLD OPEN)

PRESENT SinJustice
Dread · Fire · Witness

BLACK. Then sound.

Fire. Low and wet and patient. Beneath it: a crowd, rhythmic, certain — the sound of people who have made a decision together and are living inside it.

EXTREME CLOSE-UP.

Two eyes. Brown irises, wide and wet. Pupils contracted to points by heat. The whites faintly pink. They look straight UP — at the Jamestown sky, the same sky this person has known all their life.

Reflected in the wet surface of those eyes: flames. Orange and gold, writhing.

A tear moves from the outer corner. Evaporates before it finishes.

From somewhere in the crowd, a voice rises in Ga:

VOICE FROM THE CROWD
(in Ga, low, almost reverent)
Agoo. Agoo.

The fire breathes. The eyes stay open.

JAMESTOWN — FISHING BEACH & HIGH STREET — DAWN (1998)

1998 Innocence
Nostalgia · Dawn · Innocence

The neighborhood assembles itself from light and salt air.

JAMESTOWN. The oldest part of Accra. The Ga-Mashie heartland. The lighthouse at one end. The Atlantic at the other. Fishing canoes in primary colors — red, yellow, blue — dragged up the sand by men calling to each other in Ga. Nets hung between wooden stakes. The smell of smoked fish and charcoal.

Cinder block and old colonial walls. Paint surrendered long ago to the sea air. Laundry on lines. A woman selling kenkey from a basin on her head. A man asleep on a bench outside a provisions shop, his chalewote on the ground beside him.

A radio inside a compound: highlife, already going.

Two FISHERMEN pass in the lane.

FISHERMAN 1
(in Ga)
Mɛni yaa nɔ! !
FISHERMAN 2
(in Ga, returning the greeting)
Duromɔ ! Mii yɛ ojogbaŋŋ.

The day begins.

JAMESTOWN — BUKOM SQUARE — DAY (1998)

1998 InnocenceSin
Childhood · Play · Foreshadow

Open ground near the old boxing gym. The walls carry champions’ names in faded paint. Three boys have claimed the space with a bald football and a ferocious argument about its rules.

YOUNG WISDOM (10) — small, watchful, precise. He looks before he moves.

YOUNG Jude (10) — all elbows and noise, braver than his size suggests.

YOUNG DILLINGER (11) — already the biggest, already performing, already comfortable with being watched.

Against the far wall: three OLDER BOYS (15-16). One of them is passing a hand-rolled wee, smoking lazily, watching the younger boys with the bored authority of teenagers who own a corner.

YOUNG DILLINGER
That was goal! The ball crossed the line, chale!
YOUNG Jude
Which line? You drew the line with your own foot, you can move it anywhere!
YOUNG DILLINGER
Wisdom! You are witness — abeg, tell him!

Young Wisdom is not watching the argument. He is watching the older boys at the wall. The wee passing between them.

YOUNG WISDOM
(distracted)
Hmm. Yes. Whatever he said.
YOUNG Jude
You didn’t even look! Whose side are you?

Young Wisdom walks towards the older boys.

A NEIGHBOR WOMAN stops at the edge of the ground, laundry basin balanced on her head.

NEIGHBOR WOMAN
(in Ga and Pidgin)
Wisdom! Your mama, she dey come from the market side oh — she nearly reach the junction!

Young Wisdom grabs his shirt from the dirt. He runs — straight through the gap in the compound wall, sandals slapping the road.

Young Jude watches him go, then glances at the older boys at the wall. The wee is going around again. One of them makes brief eye contact with Jude. Then looks away.

YOUNG Jude
(to Dillinger, but still watching the older boys)
He fear him mama pass he fear God.
YOUNG DILLINGER
He fear BOTH of them, chale. Just different kind of fear.

They laugh. Young Jude looks one more time at the older boys against the wall. Then looks away.

WISDOM’S HOME — JAMESTOWN — NIGHT (1998)

1998 FaithSin
Faith · Deception · Prophecy

One room. A cord bulb. A wooden table, two chairs, a rolled mat. The Bible at the center of the table — spine cracked from daily use, a red ribbon marker. Wisdom’s MOTHER puts it there every morning.

Young Wisdom eases through the back door and shuts it with both hands. His MOTHER’s voice from the next room — humming a chorus, unpacking.

He crosses to the table. Opens the Bible at random. Fixes his face into the shape of a boy who has been here for an hour.

His eyes are slightly wrong. He and the older boys at the wall had shared something after the football game. He told himself just a little.

His MOTHER appears in the doorway. She sees her son in the lamplight over the Bible. Something in her face eases.

MOTHER
(softly)
You dey read?
YOUNG WISDOM
(not looking up)
Yes, Mama.
MOTHER
Which part?

He looks down properly. His finger has landed somewhere. He reads which verse.

YOUNG WISDOM
Romans. Six.

She nods and moves back. He exhales. Looks down properly now, searching for something convincing.

His eyes stop.

CLOSE ON THE PAGE. Romans 6:23.

"For the wages of sin is death, but the gift of God is eternal life through Jesus Christ our Lord."

He reads it once. Twice. His lips do not move the second time.

Something passes across his face that is not understanding. It is the moment just before understanding — the moment a sentence finds the crack it was looking for.

He is ten years old. He has just gotten a little high for the first time. He has just read these words.

He will not forget them.

JAMESTOWN — HIGH STREET & MARKET — MORNING (PRESENT)

PRESENT Poverty
Poverty · Routine · Decay

The same streets. Older, louder, more crowded. The lighthouse still stands. The canoes still come in. Mobile money kiosks wedged between provision shops. A car alarm nobody answers.

Jude (MID-20s) moves through the morning market with his hands in his pockets. He is lean in the way of someone who eats inconsistently. He had good posture once — it is slowly folding at the shoulders.

He stops at a price list outside a shop. Checks his wallet. The math is not working. He buys one tin of tomatoes and puts the wallet away.

He passes the old boxing gym. Through the open door: a boy maybe fourteen, working the speed bag alone. No audience. Just the work.

Jude watches him for a moment. Walks on.

He passes a wall: GOD IS GOOD painted in white. Below it, different hand: BUT WHERE IS HE? Below that, a third hand: HE DEY. HE DEY.

He reads all three. He keeps walking.

Jude & DEDE’S ROOM — JAMESTOWN COMPOUND HOUSE — EVENING

PRESENT PovertyLove
Intimacy · Fear · Tenderness

Second floor of a compound house. The room is small and carefully kept — two people have been making this work, you can see the negotiation in it. His things on one side, hers on the other. A curtain they hung together. A small electric fan on the floor that runs on the compound’s shared power line. A large plastic basin in the corner for washing — filled from the water jugs Jude carries up from the standpipe each morning.

DEDE (MID-20s) sits on the edge of the mattress, a pregnancy test held between both palms, tilting it toward the fading window light. She is a woman who does not panic easily. She is very close to panicking.

Jude leans against the far wall. He has been there for a while. The tin of tomatoes is on the table. They have not eaten.

DEDE
(measuring her voice)
The line is faint. You see how faint? Maybe it’s nothing. Maybe we need to buy another one.
Jude
It’s there, Dede. I can see it from here.

From the room above: a baby crying. Very young. The thin cry of a newborn. It goes on, then stops.

DEDE
(quietly)
That’s Korkor’s baby. She’s up there alone since Nii Ayi left.
Jude
I’m not going anywhere.
DEDE
That’s not what I meant.
Jude
I know.

He crosses the room and sits beside her. He picks up the test. The faint line is there. He turns it in his hands the way you turn a problem.

DEDE
(after a moment)
The shop is only giving me three days this week. Your site work — still nothing calling?
Jude
Supervisor said maybe next month.
DEDE
Next month.
Jude
Next month.

Outside: the call to prayer from the mosque on Kinkawe Street. A trotro horn. The neighborhood, indifferent.

DEDE
(steady, but only just)
We will figure it out, Jude. People in this compound figure things out every single day.
Jude
(standing, going to the table)
With what, Dede? Tell me with what.

She has no answer. He opens the tin of tomatoes and begins making something with what they have.

She watches his back.

The fan turns. In the corner, the water basin reflects the last of the light.

PRESENT FaithPoverty
Friendship · Warning · Divergence

Jude carries two empty water jugs toward the standpipe at the end of the street. WISDOM falls into step beside him — he moves with the unhurried ease of a man at peace with where he is going.

The old rhythm. No effort to find it.

Wisdom —

WISDOM
That IS the testimony, Jude. That’s enough.

Jude
(lifting the full jugs)
Good doesn’t pay rent, chale. I’ve been a good man my whole life. I carry water in secondhand jugs at twenty-four years old. Something is not working in this equation.

Jude
(stopped, not turning around)
I’m not looking at any road.
WISDOM
(quietly)
The Sunday offer stays.

INT 08

BAR — JAMESTOWN — NIGHT

PRESENT SinPoverty
Temptation · Charm · Seduction

Blue and purple LEDs behind rows of akpeteshie bottles. Cheap and hypnotic. The music slightly too loud for the space.

DILLINGER (LATE 20s) occupies his barstool the way a king occupies a throne. He is broad, easy, impossible to ignore. His grin arrives before the rest of him.

Jude sits across from him. The posture of a man who told himself this is just a conversation.

DILLINGER
(reading him)
You dey look like a man who’s been adding numbers that don’t balance, chale.
Jude
When do they ever balance in Jamestown?
DILLINGER
For me? Every month. Consistent. Only the “sor” part of dumsor — just light, always light.
Jude
Clean work for that?
DILLINGER
(unbothered)
Results work. The “clean” part is someone else’s concern.

He swirls his drink.

DILLINGER
You’re trying to climb a ladder they didn’t build for you, chale. They removed the bottom rungs so men like us can’t reach, and they’re upstairs telling us to hustle harder. The game is rigged. I know it, you know it. The difference is I stopped pretending it’s fair.
Jude
(direct)
Say it straight, Dillinger. What are you proposing?
DILLINGER
(leaning in, voice dropping, casual)
Old man near the lagoon. Korle Gonno side. Old man Lartey. Keeps cash in a wooden chest — doesn’t trust any bank. His nephew gave us everything. We go in after midnight, he sleeps heavy. In, find the chest, out. Twenty minutes.
Jude
No guns.
DILLINGER
Abeg. The old man sleeps like he’s already with the ancestors. We need quiet and fast. That’s you.
Jude
(after a long pause)
My cut.
DILLINGER
(slow smile)
Now we’re talking business, chale.

He names a number. Jude picks up his drink.

Jude
(quietly)
One job.
DILLINGER
One job.

They drink. The purple light turns the condensation on the glasses into something almost beautiful.

INT 09

OLD MAN LARTEY’S HOUSE — KORLE GONNO — NIGHT

PRESENT SinDeath
Silence · Theft · Collapse

Dark. Thin amber lines through shuttered windows from a streetlamp outside.

Jude moves through the room. He has rehearsed this in his mind every night for a week. His steps fall on the outer edges of his feet. He breathes through his nose.

He passes a small table. On it: a framed photograph. An old man in a suit beside a woman — both standing straight, both smiling with the careful joy of people who know this moment matters.

He does not stop.

He finds the wooden chest. The latch is simple — the trust of a man who believes in his neighbors. He opens it. Inside: bundles of cedis, rubber-banded, some wrapped in a cloth with a faded floral pattern. More than he expected.

He takes the money. All of it.

Then: a sound.

The shuffle of a heavy sleeper turning. A labored exhale. Then footsteps — slow, old, uncertain in the dark.

OLD MAN LARTEY (70s) appears in the doorway. Singlet, sleeping shorts. His eyes are not adjusted. He reaches for the light switch.

Jude moves toward the exit. But the old man has turned in the doorway.

Their eyes meet in the half-second before the light.

The old man’s hand leaves the switch and goes to his chest. His face changes — the specific, unmistakable change of a body failing from the inside. Not surprise. Something physical. Something wrong.

He makes a single sound — small, private, not meant for anyone — and his knees start to go.

Jude catches him. Purely instinct — his arms go out and he eases the old man to the floor. Lartey’s hand is pressed hard against his sternum. His breathing is wrong. Ragged, slowing, becoming something else.

Jude kneels beside him. He watches the old man’s face.

Lartey’s eyes are open. Wide open. Fixed on the ceiling. On nothing. Already somewhere else.

He stops breathing.

The room is completely silent.

Jude stays kneeling for a moment. Then he picks up a pillow that has fallen from the chair beside them. He places it, carefully, over the old man’s open eyes.

Not to kill. Not to smother. The man is already gone.

To cover what is looking at nothing.

He stands. He tucks the money under his arm. He walks to the door. He does not run. He walks, like a man with somewhere to be.

DILLINGER’S PLACE — JAMESTOWN — NIGHT (LATER)

PRESENT SinBrotherhood
Celebration · Tension · Mask

Concrete walls, a generator running, good music. The room has more people in it now — not just the crew but friends, a woman or two, the loose orbit of men who move around Dillinger’s gravity. Drinks on the table. Someone has brought food.

This is a celebration. The work is done, the money is on the table, and nobody died on their side.

KWEI (30s) and BADU (20s) — the two other crew members — are already relaxed. Kwei moves little and says less. Badu has the energy of someone who has been waiting to release it all evening.

DILLINGER’S PLACE — JAMESTOWN — MORNING (NEXT DAY)

PRESENT SinDeathBrotherhood
Dread · News · Naming

The morning after. The room is wrecked in the minor way of a good party — cups, bottles, the debris of people who stayed late. Some of the crew are still there, waking slowly.

Dillinger has a small television in the corner, antenna tilted. The morning news is on, volume low.

Jude is already awake, sitting at the table with a glass of water.

Badu, half-awake on a chair, reaches over and turns the TV volume up.

NEWS PRESENTER
(V.O., from the television)
Police in the Korle Gonno area are investigating the death of a retired civil servant whose body was found late last night by a family member. The deceased, identified as Mr. Emmanuel Lartey, aged seventy-three, appears to have been the victim of a robbery. A source at the Ghana Police Service has told this station that the cause of death is being treated as suspicious. Evidence at the scene, including the position of the body and the presence of a pillow, is consistent with suffocation—

The room wakes up completely.

Dillinger sits up straight. He looks at Jude. Jude is looking at the television.

BADU
(slowly)
Suffocation.
KWEI
(to Jude, flat)
The pillow.
Jude
(measured, not panicking)
The man died, I told you.
KWEI
What you told us and what the police are saying are two different things.

A silence. The television continues with other news. Nobody turns it off.

DILLINGER
(leaning back, thinking, then)
The police don’t know what they’re looking at. The old man’s went down. The pillow is just — it was there. They see a pillow and a dead man and they call it suffocation. It doesn’t change anything for us.

He looks at Jude with something shifting in his expression. Not accusation. Something else. Something that looks like re-evaluation.

DILLINGER
(quietly, almost to himself)
The man probably had a heart attack and Jude still put the pillow on him right? Cool as anything.
BADU
(slow grin building)
Chale. You know what that reminds me of?
KWEI
(already seeing where this is going)
Don’t.
BADU
No, no — listen. Ruthless. No feeling. Ice. You know who else was like that?

He points at Jude.

BADU
Hitler.

A beat.

KWEI
(a reluctant sound)
Badu…
BADU
I’m serious! The man didn’t even blink. The old man died right in front of him and he covered his eyes or he suffocated him, either way he still walked out with the money. That’s… chale, that’s Hitler energy.

Dillinger looks at Jude. Jude says nothing. He is looking at the table.

Dillinger starts to laugh — a low, private laugh, the kind that means something has been confirmed rather than found funny.

DILLINGER
(to Jude)
Hitler. That’s what they’re going to call you.
Jude
(a long pause, then flatly)
Call me whatever you want.

Which is exactly the answer Hitler would give.

The crew laughs. Jude picks up his water glass.

Jude.

Jude watches her wash her hands in the basin.

The water in the basin goes still.

The sun going orange and final. Fishermen mend nets. The canoes are in. Children in the shallows despite being told not to be.

Jude sits on the low sea wall. New shirt. New trainers. His eyes on the Atlantic, not seeing it.

WISDOM sits beside him without ceremony.

WISDOM
You didn’t come Sunday.
Jude
I had something.
WISDOM
Dede confirmed?
Jude
Four months.
WISDOM
(genuinely)
Chale. That’s a blessing.
Jude
Mm.

Boys run past chasing a bicycle tyre with sticks. Same game. Different boys.

WISDOM
(after a while)
I see things, Jude.
Jude
Then stop looking.
WISDOM
I can’t.
WISDOM
There is a cost to what you’re doing. Not money.
Jude
(looking at him directly)
The baby comes in five months. I don’t have room in my head for costs I can’t count. You understand me?
WISDOM
And after five months?
Jude
After five months I’ll have more room.

Wisdom looks at his friend. He knows this is a lie. He doesn’t call it that.

WISDOM
(quietly)
I’m here. When you’re done — whatever you’re doing — I’m here.
Jude
(barely audible)
I know.

The light going red. The water staying indifferent.

KANESHIE MARKET — DAY

PRESENT InnocencePoverty
Industry · Hope · Vulnerability

A fabric stall. Bolts of cloth in every pattern. The MARKET WOMAN (30s) moves through her stock with the assurance of someone who built this from nothing. A BABY rides on her back in a printed cloth carrier.

She wraps a sale, counts change twice. TSOTSOO, the neighboring trader, leans over.

TSOTSOO
(in Pidgin)
You still saving for the house? How the tin doing?
MARKET WOMAN
(not stopping, counting bolts)
Almost there, sistah. One more month, maybe two. Before the baby’s first Homowo, we’re moving. Madina side — better school zone there.
TSOTSOO
Abeg put it in a bank! Tin inside house is not safe, you hear?
MARKET WOMAN
(smiling)
The bank charges more than it gives. My tin is fine. Nobody knows where I keep it.

The baby sounds. She bounces it gently without looking — the automatic reflex of a mother running everything in parallel.

The crew. A hand-drawn map. Less talk than before.

Market woman, Kaneshie side. Fabric seller. Cash in a tin box under the bed — close to four thousand. Her cousin confirmed it.

She’s singing and humming hymns rocking the baby to sleep at the edge of the bed.

CUT TO:
EXT 21

SIDE STREET — JAMESTOWN — NIGHT

PRESENT SinDeath
Tension · Smoke · Omen

Motorcycle in shadow. The Jamestown lanes quiet at this hour — but not empty.

Twenty meters down the street, in the gap between a compound gate and a provisions shop wall: a group of TEENAGERS (5-6, ages 16-19), slouched in the darkness. One of them is passing a wee around — the same lazy circuit of a hand-rolled Marijuana that goes back to every generation of young men in this neighborhood with nothing to do after midnight.

They talk low. They are not going anywhere. They have their corner.

Jude clocks them as he and Dillinger wait. He recognizes the formation — he and Dillinger used to be those boys. He looks away.

DILLINGER sits astride the motorcycle, rolling something between his fingers. He lights it.

The smell reaches Jude.

Jude
Dillinger. What is that.
DILLINGER
(exhaling slowly)
Taking the edge off.
Jude
You said. No wee before a job. You said it yourself — first time we went out.
DILLINGER
That’s a second rule. First rule is don’t get caught. And this is a woman and a sleeping baby, Jude. The softest job we’ve done.
Jude
Don’t say that.
DILLINGER
(grinning through smoke)
You superstitious now, Hitler?

Jude doesn’t answer. He looks at the road ahead.

In the gutter, barely visible in the poor light: a baby’s PACIFIER. Pale, small. Knocked from someone’s doorstep hours ago.

Neither man sees it.

Down the street, the teenagers pass the wee again. One of them laughs at something. The sound carries in the quiet.

Dillinger takes a last pull. Drops the wee. Grinds it.

DILLINGER
Let’s go.

INT 22

MARKET WOMAN’S HOME — NIGHT

PRESENT SinInnocence
Intrusion · Theft · Alarm

Jude through the main room. His body knows.

The bedroom door ajar. He pushes it with two fingers.

The MARKET WOMAN asleep, one arm flung out. In the corner cot: the baby — a small shape under a cloth, rising and falling slowly. Deep sleep.

Jude kneels at the foot of the bed. His hand finds the tin. Pulls it out, centimeter by centimeter.

He opens it. Inside: notes wrapped in cloth, coins in a pouch. The savings of months. Organized by a woman who has counted this many times.

He takes it all.

He stands.

His elbow catches the bedside table.

The BABY BOTTLE tips. Falls.

SLOW MOTION: The bottle turns in the air, milk catching the thin window light. It hits the floor.

NORMAL SPEED.

From the cot: a sharp intake. Then the cry — high, startled, a baby wrenched from deep sleep.

The Market Woman is already sitting up. Her hand reaches for the baby. Then she sees Jude’s shape in the dark.

MARKET WOMAN
(screaming, in Ga and Pidgin)
Agoo! Agoo! Julɔ ! Somebody! He dey take my money — HELP! SOMEBODY HELP ME!

Jude is through the door.

Jude bursts into the main lane and sees the motorcycle thirty meters ahead. He sprints.

Behind him, pouring from the compound gate: the TEENAGERS from the corner. The same ones who were smoking twenty minutes ago, now animated, giving chase with the sudden purpose of young men who have been given something to do. The wee is long dropped. They are running hard, calling to each other in Ga and Pidgin.

More voices join behind them. Men woken from sleep. Women at windows. The neighborhood alarm system, human and furious.

Jude reaches the motorcycle.

Jude
(throwing himself on)
GO! Go, go —

Dillinger’s reflexes are present but fractionally slow. He stalls. Restarts. Four seconds. The teenagers close the gap.

The motorcycle launches forward. In the road ahead, the headlamp sweeps and catches —

The PACIFIER. Pale, small, in the road.

To Dillinger’s impaired brain, at this speed, in this light: something metal. A nail. A screw that will end the tyre.

He jerks LEFT.

The front wheel drops into the pothole that has lived on this street for two years and that every person in Jamestown knows to avoid.

The motorcycle bucks. Rears.

Jude GOES OVER THE BACK.

He hits the tarmac, rolls, comes to rest against the base of a compound wall. The tin box skitters away. Dillinger keeps going, the bike correcting badly around the corner and out of sight.

Jude gets up. His shoulder is wrong. His palm is bleeding.

The teenagers are very close now. He can hear them breathing.

He runs.

EXT 24

JAMESTOWN BACK LANES — CONTINUOUS

PRESENT SinDeathFaith
Gunfire · Memory · Reckoning

Jude runs through the lanes of his own neighborhood. His whole life in these streets. Every gap in every wall. But the neighborhood knows him too, and it is faster than him now.

He pulls Rascal.

He fires.

UP. Into the air. A warning shot — not at the teenagers, not at anyone. Into the sky above Jamestown.

BANG.

FLASHBACK — INSTANT — CUT: Old man Lartey’s open eyes, fixed on the ceiling. Looking at nothing.

The teenagers scatter for one second. Then reform. They know what they heard. They also know what they know about Hitler now — the name has moved through Jamestown faster than Jude realizes. It is working for him and against him simultaneously.

He runs. Fires into the air again.

BANG.

FLASHBACK — INSTANT — CUT: The child’s drawing on the trotro operator’s wall. PAPA. MAMA. ME. BABY.

He turns a corner. More ahead of him now as well as behind. They have spread through the lanes, cutting him off.

BANG. Into the air. Into the air. Warning. Warning.

FLASHBACK — INSTANT — CUT: The prayer beads on the nail. Turning in the draft.

BANG.

FLASHBACK — INSTANT — CUT: Dede at the basin. Her hands in the water. Washing past the point of clean.

BANG. Last round. Sky. Warning. Please.

FLASHBACK — INSTANT — CUT: The pregnancy test on the mattress. The faint line. Two people in a room with a fan, with water jugs in the corner, with nothing to pay for what is coming.

He squeezes the trigger.

CLICK.

Three seconds of complete silence.

Then: feet from three directions at once.

Jude stops running. He looks at Rascal. He looks ahead. He looks behind.

He lets the gun fall.

CLOSE ON RASCAL: on the Jamestown tarmac. Blued steel catching no light at all.

The feet arrive.

EXT 25

OPEN GROUND — JAMESTOWN — NIGHT

PRESENT JusticeDeathSin
Fire · Mob Justice · End

The camera will not be a voyeur here.

A circle of Jamestown people. Traders and fishermen and teenagers and mothers who came out at the alarm. People who have been robbed before. People whose patience ran out before tonight.

This is not justice. This is what fills the space where justice did not come. The difference is real. It does not stop the engine.

A TIRE. Rubber and accelerant.

Jude’s face: past fear. His eyes are open and looking up. At the Jamestown sky. At the lighthouse somewhere at the edge of it. At the same sky he has known since he was a boy running from this neighborhood to this neighborhood and back, all his life.

The orange light begins.

EXTREME CLOSE-UP: Two eyes. Reflecting flames. Wet. Looking up.

We have seen these eyes.

Now we know.

HARD CUT TO BLACK.

Sound: the fire. Then diminishing. Then only the sea. Then nothing at all.

FADE IN:
INT 26

CHURCH — JAMESTOWN — DAY (LATER)

PRESENT FaithBrotherhoodDeath
Silence · Light · Aftermath

A modest church on one of the old Jamestown streets, not far from where three boys used to play. Wooden pews, concrete floor swept clean. Louvered windows making long parallel bars of morning light across everything.

WISDOM sits in the third pew. Alone. Not praying exactly. Sitting in the way of a person who has nowhere else to bring what they are carrying.

The door opens. Slow footsteps.

DILLINGER comes down the aisle. Same clothes as the night before. Something different in his face — not guilt, exactly. Guilt would be simpler. This is the face of a man who has been handed a cost he didn’t know was in the agreement.

He sits beside Wisdom. Not across from him. Beside him.

Neither speaks. A bird in the rafters. The building breathing.

The light bar on the floor moves slowly.

WISDOM
(without looking at him)
Where is Jude?

Dillinger sits forward. Forearms on knees. He looks at his hands — the hands on the handlebars, the hands that jerked left, the hands that kept going.

He does not speak.

He exhales. Long and slow. The exhalation of a man setting something down that he will never actually be able to set down.

The silence is the answer.

Wisdom closes his eyes.

The light continues its faithful crossing of the floor.

On the wall above the altar, painted in simple letters, faded with years of sea air:

"For the wages of sin is death, but the gift of God is eternal life through Jesus Christ our Lord."

The words were always here.

Jude never came into this building.

The frame holds. Five seconds. Six. Seven.

FADE TO BLACK.
